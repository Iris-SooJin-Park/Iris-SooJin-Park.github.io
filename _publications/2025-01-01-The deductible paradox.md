I show that raising deductibles or coinsurance rates can increase optimal medical spending for some consumers in standard optimizing models. Higher cost sharing can compress the coinsurance region, inducing some consumers to jump from intermediate cost sharing to the out-of-pocket maximum where marginal price is zero. This effect is purely mechanical, operating through contract geometry rather than behavioral biases. In particular, any introduction of a positive deductible from zero generates a nonempty set of consumers who increase spending. More generally, when the initial deductible is positive, further deductible or coinsurance increases generate such switchers under conditions satisfied by the vast majority of health insurance contracts. I show that the generosity paradox exists under both perfect foresight and forward-looking behavior with uncertainty about future health needs. The results indicate that spending responses depend on whether reforms compress or expand the intermediate cost-sharing region, rather than on cost-sharing levels alone.
